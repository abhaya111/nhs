The Role of an NHS Information Analyst

Turning healthcare data into decisions that improve lives.

What is an Information Analyst?

An NHS Information Analyst collects, processes, and interprets healthcare data to support decision-making. Their work ensures hospitals run efficiently and helps improve patient outcomes across the UK.

What they actually do

Information Analysts take raw hospital data and turn it into meaningful insights. This includes analysing waiting times, patient flow, staffing levels, and treatment outcomes.

They do not treat patients directly, but their work influences almost every part of NHS decision-making.

Day-to-day responsibilities

  • Collecting and cleaning NHS healthcare data
  • Building dashboards and reports using tools like Power BI
  • Tracking hospital performance and waiting times
  • Identifying patterns and trends in patient data
  • Supporting managers with evidence-based decisions

Where they work

Information Analysts work across NHS hospital trusts, regional health boards, and national data organisations. Their role connects different parts of the healthcare system through data.

Why this role matters

The NHS depends on data to function efficiently. Without analysts, hospitals would struggle to identify problems in waiting times, staffing, and resource use.

Their work directly improves patient care by helping reduce delays and improving hospital planning.

Tools and skills used

  • Excel for data analysis and cleaning
  • SQL for working with databases
  • Power BI for dashboards and visualisation
  • Strong analytical and problem-solving skills
  • Clear communication for explaining data insights

Real-world example

If an NHS hospital has long A&E waiting times, an Information Analyst would investigate the data to find the cause. They may discover peak demand hours or staffing shortages.

They then present this information in a dashboard so managers can make changes that improve patient flow and reduce delays.
